## Overview

This section defines the design principles used when creating cards for Wizard Chess.

The goal is to create cards that are strategically interesting, easy to understand, deterministic, and faithful to the core philosophy of Wizard Chess.

Cards should introduce new decisions, not unnecessary complexity.

---

# Design Philosophy

Every card should satisfy the following principles:

- Reinforce the strategic nature of chess.
    
- Encourage meaningful decisions.
    
- Be deterministic.
    
- Be easy to understand.
    
- Be interesting without requiring excessive rules text.
    
- Remain physically playable.
    

Cards should create exceptions to the rules of chess rather than replacing chess itself.

---

# Complexity

Cards should be as simple as possible while accomplishing their intended purpose.

Whenever possible:

- Use existing mechanics.
    
- Use existing keywords.
    
- Avoid creating new rules.
    
- Avoid unnecessary conditions.
    
- Avoid unnecessary exceptions.
    

Complexity should emerge from interactions between cards rather than individual card complexity.

---

# Card Identity

Every card should have a clearly identifiable purpose.

Examples include:

- Improve movement.
    
- Protect a piece.
    
- Control the battlefield.
    
- Create positional advantages.
    
- Disrupt the opponent.
    
- Reward long-term planning.
    

A card should not attempt to fulfill multiple unrelated roles.

---

# School Identity

Cards should reinforce the identity of their School of Magic.

Examples:

**Arcane**

- Manipulation
    
- Positioning
    
- Card advantage
    
- Tactical flexibility
    

**Pyromancy**

- Aggression
    
- Destruction
    
- Board pressure
    

**Nature**

- Growth
    
- Adaptation
    
- Development
    

**Divine**

- Protection
    
- Support
    
- Stability
    

**Necromancy**

- Sacrifice
    
- Graveyard interaction
    
- Attrition
    

Whenever possible, mechanics should align with their School's identity.

---

# Piece Identity

Unit cards should complement the strengths and weaknesses of the chess piece they enhance.

Examples include:

- Pawns becoming more versatile.
    
- Knights gaining mobility or utility.
    
- Bishops improving board control.
    
- Rooks becoming stronger positional pieces.
    
- Queens gaining unique strategic options.
    
- Kings receiving defensive or supportive abilities.
    

Unit cards should encourage players to think differently about familiar chess pieces.

---

# Rarity Philosophy

Rarity should primarily represent:

- Mechanical complexity.
    
- Uniqueness.
    
- Build-around potential.
    
- Collection frequency.
    

Rarity should not directly determine power level.

A Common card should be capable of remaining useful throughout the lifetime of the game.

Legendary cards should feel unique without being mandatory.

---

# Mana Philosophy

Mana Cost should represent the strategic value and flexibility of a card rather than its raw power.

When determining a Mana Cost, consider:

- Immediate impact.
    
- Long-term value.
    
- Ease of use.
    
- Target restrictions.
    
- Board state requirements.
    
- Synergy potential.
    

Cards with restrictive play requirements may justify lower Mana Costs.

Cards with broad applicability may require higher Mana Costs.

---

# Rules Text

Rules text should:

- Be concise.
    
- Be deterministic.
    
- Be grammatically consistent.
    
- Use existing game terminology.
    
- Avoid unnecessary flavor.
    

If a mechanic requires more than a few sentences to explain, it should be reconsidered.

---

# Card Synergy

Cards should encourage players to build synergistic decks.

Synergy may exist between:

- Schools of Magic.
    
- Card types.
    
- Keywords.
    
- Chess piece types.
    
- Board positions.
    
- Game events.
    

Cards should reward planning without creating mandatory combinations.

---

# Counterplay

Every powerful card should have meaningful counterplay.

Counterplay may include:

- Positional play.
    
- Timing.
    
- Reactions.
    
- Alternative strategies.
    
- Card interactions.
    

Players should feel that they had opportunities to respond to powerful effects.

---

# Card Diversity

The card pool should support multiple viable playstyles.

Examples include:

- Aggressive
    
- Defensive
    
- Positional
    
- Control
    
- Combo
    
- Midrange
    
- Tempo
    

No single strategy should dominate the game.

---

# Expansion Philosophy

New card sets should expand existing mechanics before introducing entirely new ones.

Future sets should prioritize:

- New Unit designs.
    
- New strategic interactions.
    
- New deck archetypes.
    
- Additional support for underrepresented mechanics.
    

Core rules should remain stable whenever possible.

---

# Design Checklist

Before a card is added to the game, it should satisfy the following questions:

- Is the card deterministic?
    
- Does it reinforce the identity of its School?
    
- Does it encourage interesting chess decisions?
    
- Is the rules text concise and unambiguous?
    
- Does it create meaningful strategic choices?
    
- Does it have reasonable counterplay?
    
- Is it physically playable without digital assistance?
    
- Does it avoid introducing unnecessary complexity?
    
- Does it provide something distinct from existing cards?
    
- Is it enjoyable to both play and play against?
    

If the answer to any of these questions is "No," the card should be revised before being added to the game.
